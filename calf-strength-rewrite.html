Why Calf Strength Matters More Than You Think for Distance Runners

Ask most distance runners what muscles power their running, and they'll say quads, glutes, or hamstrings. Almost no one says "calves."

But research published in the Journal of Experimental Biology reveals something that changes everything we thought we knew about distance running: your calf muscles—specifically the soleus and gastrocnemius—are the primary drivers of running performance at distance running speeds.

The study found that for speeds up to 7 m/s (about 4:17 per mile pace—faster than most runners will ever sustain), the ankle plantarflexors contribute most significantly to vertical support forces and increases in stride length. In other words, if you want to run faster as a distance runner, your calves matter more than almost any other muscle group.

I'm going to show you why calf strength is critical for distance running performance, what research reveals about the muscle strategies your body uses at different speeds, and exactly how to build race-winning calf strength.

Key Takeaways

  • Ankle plantarflexors (calves) are the primary drivers of distance running performance up to 7 m/s (4:17/mile pace)
  • The soleus muscle is active at ALL running speeds and contributes more to distance running than any other single muscle
  • Running builds calf endurance but NOT strength—you need progressive resistance training with heavy loads
  • Adequate calf strength = 25+ single-leg calf raises; most runners can do fewer than 15
  • Weak calves are directly linked to Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis, shin splints, and calf strains
  • Stronger calves improve running economy, tendon stiffness, and force production with each stride

Research Summary — What Studies Actually Show

For years, distance running training focused on building aerobic capacity, improving form, and strengthening the "big" muscles—quads, glutes, hamstrings. Calves were an afterthought, assumed to get strong enough from running itself.

Then researchers at the University of Melbourne conducted a landmark biomechanics study that changed our understanding of which muscles actually power distance running.

Using sophisticated musculoskeletal modeling, they analyzed exactly which muscles contribute to running at speeds from slow jogging all the way to maximal sprinting. The study measured the forces developed by individual leg muscles across this entire speed spectrum.

The critical finding: there's a speed threshold at 7 m/s (approximately 4:17 per mile pace) where the body's muscle recruitment strategy fundamentally shifts.

Below 7 m/s: Ankle-Dominant Strategy

At distance running speeds—everything slower than 4:17/mile pace—the research found that runners primarily increase speed by increasing stride length, not stride frequency. And stride length at these speeds is generated primarily by the ankle plantarflexors (soleus and gastrocnemius).

These muscles push harder against the ground to generate greater vertical support forces, propelling the body upward and forward with each stride. The stronger your ankle plantarflexors, the more force you can generate, the longer your stride length, and the faster you run.

Let's put this threshold in perspective for distance runners:

  • 5K race pace (18:00 finish): 5:48/mile = 4.6 m/s — Ankle-dominant
  • 10K race pace (40:00 finish): 6:26/mile = 4.1 m/s — Ankle-dominant
  • Half marathon pace (1:30 finish): 6:52/mile = 3.9 m/s — Ankle-dominant
  • Marathon pace (3:00 finish): 6:52/mile = 3.9 m/s — Ankle-dominant
  • Easy training pace: 8:00-10:00/mile = 2.7-3.4 m/s — Ankle-dominant

Unless you're running mile repeats at sub-5:00 pace, you're operating in ankle-dominant territory. Your calves are the primary performance drivers.

Above 7 m/s: Hip-Dominant Strategy

Above the 7 m/s threshold—at true sprinting speeds—the strategy shifts. At very high velocities, the ankle plantarflexors can't generate force quickly enough because they're shortening at high speeds with less time to produce force.

At sprint speeds, the body compensates by recruiting hip muscles (iliopsoas, gluteus maximus, hamstrings) to swing the legs more rapidly through the air, increasing stride frequency rather than stride length. This is why sprinters have massive glutes and hip flexors—they operate above the threshold where hip muscles dominate.

But distance runners? We live below that threshold, in calf-muscle territory.

The Soleus: The Endurance Workhorse

Within the calf complex, the research identified something particularly important: the soleus muscle (the deeper calf muscle underneath the visible gastrocnemius) is active at ALL running speeds and contributes more to distance running than any other muscle.

Why does the soleus dominate? It's composed primarily of Type I (slow-twitch) muscle fibers built for sustained, repetitive contractions. The soleus is literally designed for what distance running demands: firing thousands of times per run without fatigue.

The gastrocnemius has more Type II (fast-twitch) fibers and becomes increasingly important at higher speeds, but for distance runners, the soleus is the workhorse that powers every single stride.

Supporting Research on Calf Strength and Performance

Additional research reinforces the critical role of calf strength in running performance:

Running economy: Studies show that calf strength—particularly the ability to generate high forces quickly—correlates strongly with better running economy. Research in the European Journal of Applied Physiology found that runners with stiffer Achilles tendons (built through heavy calf training) had significantly better running economy.

Elastic energy return: Research in the Journal of Experimental Biology demonstrated that the Achilles tendon acts as a spring during running, storing and releasing elastic energy with each stride. Stronger calf muscles and stiffer tendons improve this energy return, leading to more efficient running—you literally bounce better.

Injury prevention: Multiple studies link weak calf strength to common running injuries including Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis, shin splints, and calf strains. Insufficient calf strength means the Achilles tendon absorbs more stress than it can handle, foot mechanics alter to increase plantar fascia strain, and the anterior tibialis must work harder to compensate.

What This Means for You as a Runner

Who This Applies To

Calf strengthening is critical for virtually all distance runners, but it's especially important if you:

  • Race distances from 5K to marathon where you're operating below the 7 m/s threshold
  • Have a history of Achilles pain, plantar fasciitis, or shin splints
  • Notice your form deteriorating significantly during the final miles of long runs or races
  • Can't perform 25+ single-leg calf raises with bodyweight alone
  • Currently don't do any calf-specific resistance training beyond running
  • Want to improve running economy and performance without just adding more mileage

Most runners assume their calves are strong because they run frequently. But running builds calf endurance, not maximum strength. To actually increase muscle strength, research shows you need to load the muscle with forces significantly higher than it experiences during normal running.

How to Apply the Findings

The research points to a clear strategy for building performance-enhancing calf strength:

Target both calf muscles appropriately. The soleus and gastrocnemius require different training approaches. Straight-leg calf raises emphasize the gastrocnemius, while bent-knee calf raises isolate the soleus. Distance runners need both, with emphasis on soleus work.

Use progressive resistance, not just high reps. Once you can perform 25+ bodyweight single-leg calf raises, you're building endurance you already have from running. To build strength, you need to add significant resistance—work up to 50-100+ pounds on single-leg calf raises.

Include eccentric training. Eccentric calf strength (controlling the lengthening phase) is critical for Achilles tendon health and injury prevention. Research on Achilles tendinopathy consistently shows eccentric calf training as the most effective rehabilitation and prevention strategy.

Train frequency that works. Unlike other muscle groups, calves can handle higher frequency training (2-3x per week) because they're accustomed to working daily during running. However, heavy calf work will cause soreness initially—don't schedule it the day before hard workouts or long runs until you've adapted.

Common Mistakes

Even runners who commit to calf training often make mistakes that undermine results:

Only doing bodyweight calf raises. The most common error. Doing 50, 100, or even 300 bodyweight calf raises builds endurance, not strength. Once you can do 25+ single-leg calf raises with bodyweight, you must add resistance. Progressive overload with actual weight is non-negotiable for building strength.

Partial range of motion. Many runners do calf raises without full range—they don't lower their heel below the step level or don't rise all the way onto their toes. Full range of motion from heel drop to maximum toe raise is critical for both strength development and Achilles tendon health.

Neglecting the soleus. Most runners only do straight-leg calf raises, which primarily target the gastrocnemius. But research shows the soleus is the endurance workhorse for distance running. You must include bent-knee calf raises to specifically strengthen the soleus.

Skipping eccentric work. The eccentric phase (lowering under control) is where Achilles tendon adaptations occur. Research on Achilles tendinopathy shows that eccentric training is the gold standard for both prevention and rehabilitation. Never rush through the lowering phase—make it slow and controlled.

Expected Outcomes

Based on research protocols and clinical experience with hundreds of runners, here's a realistic timeline:

  • Weeks 1-2: Initial soreness as muscles adapt to new loading. Exercises feel challenging. You'll notice improved muscle activation.
  • Weeks 3-4: Soreness decreases. You'll feel stronger—weights that felt heavy initially now feel manageable. Running may start feeling more "springy."
  • Weeks 6-8: Measurable strength improvements. You'll be able to add 20-30 lbs to your single-leg calf raises. Form holds up better on long runs. Ground contact time may decrease.
  • Weeks 8-12: Substantial improvements in calf strength and power. Running economy improvements may become measurable. Your ability to maintain form when fatigued is noticeably better. If you had chronic Achilles or calf issues, symptoms typically resolve significantly during this phase.
  • 3+ months: With continued training 2-3x per week, these adaptations become your new baseline. Injury risk stays low, and mechanical efficiency improvements persist.

The key is consistency and progressive overload. Research shows that sporadic calf training or stalling at bodyweight exercises doesn't produce meaningful strength adaptations.

Strength Training Recommendations (Evidence-Based)

Key Muscles to Target

Your calf strengthening program needs to target three key areas based on research findings:

Soleus (Primary Focus): The deeper calf muscle that crosses only the ankle joint. Composed primarily of Type I fibers built for endurance. Active at all running speeds and the primary contributor to distance running performance. Trained with bent-knee exercises.

Gastrocnemius (Secondary Focus): The visible calf muscle that crosses both the knee and ankle joints. Has more Type II fibers and becomes more important at higher speeds. Trained with straight-leg exercises.

Achilles Tendon Complex (Critical): The tendon that connects your calf muscles to your heel bone. Tendon stiffness directly impacts running economy through elastic energy return. Strengthened through heavy loading and eccentric training.

Research-Backed Exercises

These five exercises directly address the strength and power demands identified in biomechanics research:

1. Heavy Single-Leg Calf Raises
Stand on one leg on a step or raised platform with your heel hanging off the edge. Hold a heavy dumbbell or wear a weighted vest. Rise up onto your toes as high as possible, achieving maximum plantarflexion. Hold at the top for one full second to eliminate momentum. Lower down slowly over 3-4 seconds until your heel drops well below the step level, stretching your calf and Achilles. This is ONE rep—quality and range of motion matter more than speed.

Why it works: Loads your gastrocnemius and soleus with forces higher than running, building maximum strength. The single-leg pattern mimics the unilateral demands of running. Progressive resistance creates strength adaptations, not just endurance.

Progression: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps per leg. When you can complete 12 reps with excellent form, add 5-10 lbs. Work up to holding 50-100+ pounds over time.

2. Eccentric-Focused Calf Raises
Stand on both feet on a step. Rise up onto both toes to the top position. Lift one foot completely off the ground. Now lower down slowly on the single standing leg, taking a full 5 seconds to descend until your heel drops below the step level. Place your other foot down and use both legs to rise back up to the starting position. Repeat on the same leg for all reps before switching.

Why it works: Specifically strengthens the eccentric phase of the calf contraction and progressively loads the Achilles tendon. Research on Achilles tendinopathy consistently shows eccentric training as the most effective intervention. The controlled lowering under load builds tendon resilience and prevents injury.

Progression: 3 sets of 10-12 reps per leg. Progress by adding weight held in your hands or wearing a weighted vest, not by increasing speed.

3. Bent-Knee Soleus Raises
Sit on a bench or chair with your feet flat on the ground and knees bent at 90 degrees. Place a heavy weight (barbell with plates, heavy dumbbells, or a loaded sandbag) across your knees. Keep your knees bent throughout the entire movement—this is critical. Rise up onto the balls of your feet as high as possible, then lower with control. Don't rest at the bottom position—maintain constant tension on the soleus.

Why it works: The bent-knee position takes the gastrocnemius out of the movement, isolating the soleus—the primary muscle for distance running. Research shows the soleus contributes more to distance running than any other muscle, yet most runners never train it specifically.

Progression: 3 sets of 15-20 reps. Work up to 100+ pounds on your knees. When that weight becomes manageable, add a pause at the top of each rep.

4. Explosive Pogo Hops
Stand with feet hip-width apart and minimal knee bend. Hop continuously using only your ankles—imagine you're a pogo stick. Keep your knees relatively straight throughout, forcing your calves to do all the work. Minimize ground contact time while trying to maximize hop height. Land on the balls of your feet and immediately spring back up.

Why it works: Develops the elastic qualities and reactive strength critical for the stretch-shortening cycle during running. Research shows that tendon stiffness and rate of force development directly impact running economy. This exercise trains the "bounce" you need for efficient running.

Progression: 3 sets of 20-30 hops. Progress to single-leg pogo hops once the bilateral version is easy. Add a weighted vest for additional challenge.

5. Farmer's Walk on Toes
Hold heavy dumbbells or kettlebells at your sides (start with 25-50% of your body weight per hand). Rise up onto your toes and stay there. Walk forward on your toes for distance or time, never allowing your heels to touch the ground. Keep your torso upright and core engaged throughout.

Why it works: Combines calf strength with endurance under sustained load. The walking pattern transfers well to the repetitive nature of distance running. This exercise builds the fatigue resistance your calves need to maintain form in the final miles of races.

Progression: 3 sets of 30-60 seconds or 40-50 yards. Progress by increasing weight, not speed or distance initially.

Weekly Structure That Works

Research-based progression for building race-winning calf strength:

Weeks 1-2 (Adaptation Phase):

  • Heavy single-leg calf raises: 3 sets of 12-15 reps per leg (moderate weight)
  • Eccentric calf raises: 3 sets of 10 reps per leg (bodyweight or light added weight)
  • Bent-knee soleus raises: 3 sets of 15 reps (moderate weight)
  • Frequency: 2x per week (Monday and Thursday, for example)
  • Focus: Learn movement patterns, establish full range of motion, manage initial soreness
  • Timing: After easy runs, never before hard workouts or long runs

Weeks 3-4 (Building Phase Begins):

  • Heavy single-leg calf raises: 3-4 sets of 10-12 reps per leg (increase weight)
  • Eccentric calf raises: 3 sets of 12 reps per leg (add weight, maintain 5-second lowering)
  • Bent-knee soleus raises: 3 sets of 20 reps (increase weight significantly)
  • Add: Pogo hops 2-3 sets of 20 reps
  • Frequency: 2-3x per week
  • Focus: Progressive overload—add 5-10 lbs when you hit the top of the rep range

Weeks 5-8 (Maximum Strength Phase):

  • Heavy single-leg calf raises: 4 sets of 8-10 reps per leg (heavy weight, 50+ lbs added)
  • Eccentric calf raises: 3 sets of 12 reps per leg (weighted)
  • Bent-knee soleus raises: 3 sets of 20 reps (80-100+ lbs)
  • Pogo hops: 3 sets of 20-30, progress to single-leg
  • Farmer's walk on toes: 3 sets of 45 seconds
  • Frequency: 3x per week
  • Focus: Continue progressive overload, maintain excellent form under heavy loads

Week 9+ (Maintenance Phase):

  • Continue all exercises with heavy loads
  • Frequency: 2x per week (sufficient to maintain adaptations)
  • Focus: This becomes part of your permanent training routine
  • During race season: Reduce to 1x per week, maintain heavy loads, reduce total volume by 25%

Sample Strength-Focused Workout (20 minutes):

  1. Heavy Single-Leg Calf Raises: 4 sets × 10 reps each leg (2-3 min rest between sets)
  2. Eccentric Calf Raises: 3 sets × 12 reps each leg (2 min rest)
  3. Bent-Knee Soleus Raises: 3 sets × 20 reps (90 sec rest)
  4. Pogo Hops: 3 sets × 25 reps (90 sec rest)

Sample Endurance-Focused Workout (15 minutes):

  1. Bent-Knee Soleus Raises: 3 sets × 25 reps (heavy weight, 90 sec rest)
  2. Single-Leg Calf Raises: 3 sets × 25 reps per leg (bodyweight, 60 sec rest)
  3. Farmer's Walk on Toes: 3 sets × 45-60 seconds (heavy, 90 sec rest)

Progression Guidance

Progress to the next level when you can complete all prescribed sets and reps with excellent form and 1-2 reps left in reserve:

  • Add weight systematically: Increase by 5-10 lbs for single-leg exercises, 10-20 lbs for bilateral exercises when you hit the top of the rep range with good form
  • Never sacrifice range of motion: Full heel drop below the step and maximum toe raise on every single rep. Partial range undermines both strength gains and tendon adaptations
  • Control the eccentric: Always lower slowly (3-5 seconds). This is where Achilles tendon strengthening occurs
  • Progress plyometrics cautiously: Add volume (more reps/sets) before adding complexity (single-leg variations) or external load

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my calves are too weak?

Try this test: stand on one leg on a step with your heel hanging off. Perform single-leg calf raises with full range of motion (heel drops below step, rise all the way onto toes). Count maximum reps before failure. Research suggests distance runners need 25+ reps for adequate strength. If you can do fewer than 15 reps per leg, your calves are significantly weak and likely limiting your performance.

Won't running itself make my calves strong enough?

No. Running builds calf endurance, not strength. Research shows that to increase muscle strength, you must load the muscle with forces significantly higher than it experiences during normal activity. Running provides high repetitions but relatively low force. It's like doing 10,000 bicep curls with 5-pound weights—you'll build endurance, but not maximum strength or power. You need progressive resistance training with heavy loads.

How long until calf training improves my running performance?

Research protocols show measurable running economy improvements after 6-8 weeks of consistent heavy calf training (2-3x per week). However, you may notice subjective improvements sooner—better bounce, maintained form late in runs, less calf fatigue—within 3-4 weeks. Performance improvements in races typically become apparent after 8-12 weeks as strength and tendon adaptations fully transfer to running mechanics.

Will heavy calf training make my legs bulky?

No. Calf training for distance runners focuses on strength and power, not muscle size. The low rep ranges (8-12 reps) with heavy loads build neural strength and tendon stiffness without significant hypertrophy. Additionally, the high-volume endurance training you're doing (running) actively suppresses muscle growth through the interference effect. You'll build stronger, more powerful calves without adding meaningful mass.

I have chronic Achilles pain. Should I still do calf exercises?

Eccentric calf training is actually the gold standard treatment for Achilles tendinopathy based on extensive research. However, if you currently have acute Achilles pain, consult a physical therapist before starting heavy loading. In most cases, a graduated eccentric calf strengthening program (starting with bodyweight, progressing to heavy loads over weeks) is exactly what resolves chronic Achilles issues. The key is appropriate progression and monitoring symptoms.

Summary

Research is unambiguous: for distance runners operating below 7 m/s (slower than 4:17/mile pace), the ankle plantarflexors—your calf muscles—are the primary drivers of running performance. The soleus muscle in particular is active at all running speeds and contributes more to distance running than any other single muscle.

Running itself builds calf endurance but not the maximum strength needed for optimal performance. You need progressive resistance training with heavy loads—work up to 50-100 lbs on single-leg calf raises, emphasize the soleus with bent-knee exercises, and include eccentric work for Achilles tendon health.

The biomechanics are clear: stronger calves generate more force with each push-off, improve stride length, enhance tendon stiffness for better elastic energy return, and directly improve running economy. Research shows these adaptations occur within 6-12 weeks of consistent training.

Start with the five exercises outlined above—heavy single-leg calf raises, eccentric raises, bent-knee soleus work, plyometric hops, and loaded carries.
